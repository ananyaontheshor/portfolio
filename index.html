law, data, and the gap between them

i started with a BBA LL.B from Army Law College, Pune, which taught me to read systems for where they break: ambiguity, loopholes, the gap between what a rule says and what it does. then i moved into data and analytics, because i wanted to build the systems, not just argue about them.

that pivot showed up fastest at Tata nexarc, where a 5-month marketing analytics internship had me automating cohort revenue pipelines across 5,700+ transaction records, building churn models from logistics data, and consolidating 50+ campaign KPIs into self-updating dashboards. currently i'm a product management intern at CredgeSol.ai, an AI decision governance startup, presenting the Credge Clarity Engine pitch to internal stakeholders.

outside of work i write, take photographs, paint badly on purpose, and occasionally publish poetry. i used to run a public poetry page. i think the creative habits and the analytical ones feed each other more than people expect.

things i've shipped

four projects, expanded below. each one sits at a different point of the law/data/product triangle. hover for a little tilt.

133klistings analyzed
5xhigher bestseller rate, KU titles
25xmore reviews, KU vs paid
47%of listings have zero reviews

a self-directed analysis of 133,000+ Amazon Kindle book listings, examining whether pricing model — Kindle Unlimited status, price tier, and genre — structurally determines discoverability. it does: KU titles significantly outperform paid titles on bestseller rank and reader engagement across every genre category, while titles priced over $15 are effectively invisible to algorithmic discovery.

the more interesting finding was the shape of the system underneath that gap: a two-track discovery model where algorithmic ranking favors low-cost titles while editorial curation skews toward premium content — creating a structural disadvantage for independent and niche authors regardless of quality.

tools: Python, pandas, matplotlib, seaborn, Tableau · dataset: Kaggle, 2023

5L+documents indexed
5specialised agent endpoints
4source types: SC, HC, SEBI, RBI

existing MCP connectors for Indian legal data exposed 5 lakh+ documents across the Supreme Court, High Courts, SEBI, and RBI — but offered no reasoning or synthesis layer for practitioners. NyayaAI is my answer: an agentic legal research platform built on the Claude API and MCP, with five specialised agent endpoints for case law search, document retrieval, judgment analysis, compliance checking, and act-to-judgment correlation, each routing through Claude to a legal data hunter MCP.

built and deployed full-stack using Claude Code as an agentic coding assistant, with PDF and DOCX ingestion support and structured, source-cited outputs from plain language queries. the legal background did the heavy lifting on architecture: knowing what a lawyer actually needs from a research tool, versus what a generic search wrapper gives them.

stack: Claude API, MCP, Claude Code · deployed on Railway

9.5k+shipment records
3primary churn signals identified

built during my Tata nexarc internship: engineered features across SLA compliance, LSP (logistics service provider) performance, order sequence, and key account status from 9,500+ anonymised B2B logistics shipment records. trained a logistic regression classifier to predict churn, and identified first-order customers, SLA non-compliance, and LSP selection as the primary signals driving monthly reorder behaviour.

the output wasn't just a model — it was a Power BI dashboard visualising customer-level churn risk scores with LSP-level performance breakdowns and early-intervention recommendations for new customer onboarding, meant to be used by the team, not just read once.

tools: Python, scikit-learn, Power BI · source: Tata nexarc internship data

this one's pure product instinct with no client brief — i noticed the gap, then built the thing i actually wanted to use.
