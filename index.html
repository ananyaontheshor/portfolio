law, data, and the gap between them

i started with a BBA LLB, which taught me to read systems for where they break: ambiguity, loopholes, the gap between what a rule says and what it does. then i moved into data and analytics, because i wanted to build the systems, not just argue about them.

that combination is unusual, and i've learned to treat it as the point rather than the complication. at CredgeSol.ai, an AI decision governance startup, i work on the Credge Clarity Engine — explaining and presenting how AI decisions should be made legible to the people affected by them.

outside of work i write, take photographs, paint badly on purpose, and occasionally publish poetry. i used to run a public poetry page. i think the creative habits and the analytical ones feed each other more than people expect.

02 — projects
02 / projects

things i've shipped

three projects, expanded below. each one sits at a different point of the law/data/product triangle. hover for a little tilt.

at CredgeSol.ai, i'm building and presenting the Credge Clarity Engine deck to internal stakeholders and external audiences. the product addresses a real gap: companies deploying AI decision systems often can't explain, in plain language, why a system decided what it decided. my work covers the pitch narrative, stakeholder messaging, and a research spotlight slide drawing on independent work i co-authored on AI governance.

the hardest part of this hasn't been the deck itself — it's been keeping a clean line between the product and my own research, so the pitch never overstates what's been built versus what's been studied.

role: product management intern · stage: internal v7, early days

NyayaAI is a multi-agent legal research platform i built and deployed, covering Indian court judgments, SEBI orders, and RBI notifications, alongside AI-powered contract review and compliance checks. the legal background did the heavy lifting here: knowing what a lawyer actually needs from a research tool, versus what a generic search wrapper gives them.

it's live and queryable, not a slide deck. that distinction matters to me — i wanted to ship something a lawyer could actually open and use.

stack: multi-agent architecture, deployed on Railway
visit nyayaai →

every other medium has a tracker with personality — Letterboxd for film, Goodreads for books, Trakt for TV. games never got one. SideQuest is my answer: a personal game tracker with an arcade/CRT visual identity, built on FastAPI and Jinja2, with RAWG API integration for game discovery.

this one's pure product instinct with no client brief — i noticed the gap, then built the thing i actually wanted to use.

stack: FastAPI, Jinja2, PostgreSQL, deployed on Railway
